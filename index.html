This diary has been running since September 15, 2019. It works as both an archive and a living record of how my relationship with films changes over time.

The scale expands the usual star ratings into 100 values. Although the scores are numerical, they don't follow strict mathematical rules—72 is not 7.2 and cannot be floored to 7/10 or 3½ stars. The values 71–80 are just a way to distribute 4-star movies.

Why aren't star ratings enough? A movie scored 80 means significantly more to me than one rated 61, but because there aren't enough stars (pigeonhole principle), they end up with just half a star difference, which I can't endure. Nothing can replace an actual review, but this scale helps me broadly quantify my feelings towards a film.

How do I score so precisely? The scores are estimates and I don't mull over 1–2 points often. Any distinction or preference between films only becomes meaningful when the difference in score is sizeable (3 points). Also, scoring becomes intuitive with time, especially after 1500+ movies.
